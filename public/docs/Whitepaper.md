# alexandria.

## what's left for us?

Intelligence is being industrialised — made cheap, made fast, made at scale. Add robots for the body, and you get the claim this whole decade runs on: whatever you do, a machine will do, and do it better. You don't have to believe it yet. The question it forces stands either way.

And it isn't a rhetorical question. When a machine can do everything you do, and do it better, what are people *for*?

This paper answers that, from the ground up — and the answer is the whole of what Alexandria is. What's actually happening. What a person really brings. Where a human still gets paid for it. Why the one part that wins is in more danger than it looks. What to do about it, for yourself. And what it adds up to if enough people do.

It's one argument, start to finish. Each part answers the question the part before it left open. Read the headings alone and you'll have the shape of it.

*This is the full argument. For a shorter, more personal version, read [the letter](/docs/letter.pdf).*

## the premise — whatever you do, a machine will do it better.

Start with the part that isn't really in dispute anymore.

In 1951, Alan Turing — one of the founders of modern computing — said that once machines could think, we should "expect the machines to take control." He treated it as a question of when, not if. Seventy-five years later, the field largely agrees with him. In 2023, Geoffrey Hinton, whose work underlies nearly every ai system running today, left Google so he could say plainly that ai is on track to beat humans at most things, perhaps everything, and that we are not ready for it.

This isn't hype. ai runs on the same basic principle as the brain: networks of artificial neurons that strengthen their connections through experience, the way the neurons in your head do. We didn't stumble onto that — we copied it from biology on purpose, and it worked. And once you take a principle out of biology and rebuild it in silicon, it stops obeying biology's limits. A bird is remarkable, but no bird flies at three times the speed of sound. A jet isn't a better bird; it's a different thing with a higher ceiling. ai is that same move applied to thinking, and robotics is that move applied to the body. The ceiling rises with every release, and no law of physics says it stops anywhere near you.

You're free to bet against this. Just know what you're betting against: more or less every government, every large company, and most of the smartest people alive, all pushing the same way with all their money. There is no wall — no missing piece, no law, no obstacle — standing between here and there. It's simply where almost all the effort is pointed.

So take the strong version, because that's where it gets interesting: **ai and robots will match or beat humans at everything humans do.** Everything from here is about what's left when that's true.

## what a human is — four things you do, one thing you are.

To work out what's left, count what a person actually brings. It's a short list: four capabilities and one property.

The four capabilities are **intelligence, empathy, strength, and dexterity** — brain, heart, legs, and hands. That's the whole list, because everything a person does is either mind or body; there's no third place for a skill to live. Anything you might add — creativity, leadership, taste — breaks down into some mix of those four when you look closely.

And all four are getting cheap, one after another. Strength went first, a century ago, when machines outmatched any workforce. Intelligence is going now — the memo, the model, the analysis. Dexterity is next, as robots gain good hands. Even empathy goes: a model trained on billions of conversations becomes warm enough that most people, most of the time, accept the warmth without looking behind it. Much of what feels like heart is really intelligence in another form.

The fifth thing is different. It isn't something you *do* — it's something you *are*: the simple fact that **you** were the one who did it. There's a plain word for this: a *property*. Not something you own, like a house — a property the way being wet is a property of water, or being heavy is a property of lead: a built-in fact that's just true of the thing, no matter what. Being you is your property. No one else can have it, and nothing you do earns it — it's simply the fact that the one who did it was you.

Here's the clearest way to see it. Imagine aliens arrive, and every one of them is better than you at all four capabilities — sharper, stronger, more precise, more caring. If the alien opened a restaurant, would you eat there? If it were your therapist, would you book the session? For most meals and most sessions, yes — it would be better. So the real question is what you'd still do *yourself*, knowing the alien does it better.

You'd still cook dinner for someone you love. Still hug your child. Still write the toast for your friend's wedding. Still stand at your father's grave for a while. The alien can have every capability there is. It can't be one of us — and for a whole category of things, that's the only thing that matters.

Picture the alien weaving a blanket identical to your grandmother's. Same wool, same pattern, no test could tell them apart. It still isn't the same blanket. The value was never in the wool; it was in whose hands made it. The moment a machine makes it, the thing you cared about is gone. And it works at two levels: *a human made this* is worth something, and *this specific human made this* — your grandmother, your friend, you — is worth far more.

So that's the shape of what's left. The four capabilities compete on the thing that gets made, and there the alien wins. The fifth competes on who made it, and there the alien can't compete at all — because the moment it does the thing, the thing stops being what you valued. That's the one part of you that can't be copied. The next question is whether anyone will pay for it.

## where humans get paid — clear the bar, then be the human who did it.

The alien beats you at all four capabilities. So what will anyone pay a human to do?

Start with why this is hard. The work in any job — any job — is just those four capabilities in some mix: intelligence, empathy, strength, dexterity. The alien has more of all four. So on the work itself, head to head, you lose every time. "Follow your passion" doesn't change that. To find where a human still gets paid, you have to find the games where the job *isn't* settled on the work alone — and you find them by the shape of the game. A restaurant is the easiest place to look, because a restaurant isn't one job — it's a stack of them: prep, cook, plate, serve, host, clean, manage.

**Maximisation games — hand them over.** Here more is always better, with no ceiling: speed getting the orders out, volume at the dish station, squeezing the recipe for every last cent. Every scrap of extra capability pays, forever — and the work is just the four capabilities, every one of which the alien has more of. It wins outright, and a human in the seat is only a bottleneck. Don't play these for money. Give them to the alien and move up.

**Threshold games — clear the bar, and it's a tie.** Most of the economy is here. There's a hard line, and nothing above it counts: the kitchen is clean enough to pass inspection, the food is safe, the contract is valid, the car got you there in one piece. A spotless kitchen earns you nothing extra. The alien clears the bar — but so do you, with the alien's help. And once you've both cleared it, you're even on the work, and only one question is left: does anyone care that a *human* did it? The alien waiter remembers every name, reads every table, never has an off night — most nights you'd take it. But the anniversary dinner, or the place where the owner knows your family, and you want the person. Same job, same bar, opposite answer.

**Diminishing games — climb to the flat, then it's the same tie.** Part bar, part climb. More capability keeps adding real value, but the gains taper, until past a point the person you're serving can no longer tell the difference: a 99% sear and a 100% sear are the same sear to the one eating it. While the gains are still climbing, the alien is simply better. Where it has flattened, you're as good as it is, and it's back to whether a human did it. You tie wherever you can reach the flat part, leaning on the alien to get there — and you fall behind where you can't, above all in real time: the live sear, the conversation, the performance, where there's no pausing to use the tool.

Then there are games decided by rule, not by work — games that declare what kind of player is allowed. Sport is the clean case: the event exists *because* a human is doing it, so a robot sprinter doesn't win the race, it ends it. The rule cuts the other way too — a drone race bars humans. And chess is the proof that caring who plays is not nostalgia. Engines passed the best human in 1997 and now sit far beyond any human's reach, yet human chess has never been bigger, and nobody pays to watch two engines play. The unbeatable machine has sat there for nearly thirty years and made the human game *more* valuable, not less.

So every game you can still win ends at the same place: even on the work, and the decision falls to whether someone cares that a human did it. What decides that?

Two numbers, and people constantly blur them. The first is the **cost difference**: how much more you cost than the alien to do the same job — your time, your wage, the fact that you sleep. The second is the **premium**: how much extra people will pay *because* a human did it. You win when the premium beats the cost difference. Keep the two on opposite sides of the table; mixing them is where the thinking goes wrong.

The premium has levers. The biggest is closeness: a stranger's blanket is worth barely more than a machine's, and your grandmother's is worth almost anything — the tighter the bond between maker and receiver, the higher the premium. Which means you don't have to be good enough for the whole world. Not good enough to compete in your field? Be good enough for your town. Not your town? Your family. Not your family? Yourself — at the centre of the circle you always clear the bar, because the audience is you. The grandmother isn't a professional knitter. The blanket she made is the most valuable one her granddaughter owns.

The other lever is culture — and hold onto this one, because the whole thing comes back to it at the end. A society chooses what it cares about. One that values the human hand pays for it; one that doesn't automates and pockets the difference. The premium isn't fixed by physics; it's a decision a whole culture makes. That decision can be moved, and moving it is half of what Alexandria is for.

The cost difference has a lever too, and it's the move to walk away with. The alien has been your rival across the table this whole time — but the same ai is also a tool in your hand. It's both at once. As your rival, it competes for the job; as your tool, you aim it at the work and take the credit, the way every tool has always worked — the chef didn't grow the wheat, the writer didn't make the pen, and the cook with the better oven still gets the credit for the meal. So in every tied game the winning play is the same: use the ai to clear the bar cheaply, then collect the premium for being the human who did. Just mind the direction of the arrow: *you give the intent, it does the work.* The moment it gives the intent and you do the work, you've swapped places — and you're the tool.

Last thing, because it gets confused constantly. All of this is about *money* — what someone will pay a human for. It says nothing about what you should *do*. Cook, write, run, and paint for free for the rest of your life, simply because you want to.

## but this was never really about money.

That last point is the loose thread, and pulling it unravels the whole money argument. Everything up to here was about getting paid — because that's the version nobody can wave away: even on cold market logic, when the machine does everything better, a human still has one thing to sell, being the one who did it. True. And it's the easy part.

But if a machine can do your job, your admin, your whole working life — then "what will I be paid for" stops being the real question. The real one is older and heavier: what is a life *for*, when nothing needs you to do anything at all?

That's the question under all of this, and it's the reason the company exists. There's a name for what's coming — the *singularity*: the point where machines can out-think us at everything we do. The crisis it brings isn't economic — it's existential. Not what to do for a living. What to do with a life. So the rest of this paper isn't really about staying employable. It's about staying a *person* — awake, free, still yourself — when the easiest thing in the world will be to quietly stop being one.

Keep the money argument. It got us in the door, and it's the floor under everything that follows. But from here the stakes are bigger than a paycheck. What's left for us was never just what you'll be paid for. It's whether there's still a you in there to pay.

## it only counts if there's a developed you.

Look back at what just happened. Read it as being about money or about meaning — either way, every road through it ended in the same place: the part that's left is the part that is *you*, your property, the fifth thing. That's the answer to "what's left for us." It's also where most people stop, satisfied. Don't. The answer has a catch, and the catch is the rest of this paper.

Your property only counts if there's a *developed you* behind it. Look again at how you actually win a game: you choose which games to play, you aim the tool, and you judge when the work is finally right. Every one of those is a decision, and every decision comes out of how you think — your judgment, your taste, your sense of what matters. A vague, borrowed, half-formed version of you gives vague instructions and gets mediocre results back. A sharp, developed version gives precise direction and gets excellent results. The tool is the same in both hands. The person holding it is not.

So now we can name the thing precisely. Out of everything you are, the part that does the choosing and the judging — the part you can actually *develop* — is how you think, put into words: your reasoning, your positions, your taste, the way you weigh one thing against another. Call it your **inner reasoning**. It isn't all of you — your warmth, your presence, your gut instincts are real and matter — but it's the root, because it's the part you can deliberately work on, and working on it lifts everything else. Think more clearly and you direct the tool better, so you clear more bars. Think more clearly and you understand people better, so your warmth lands truer. Your inner reasoning is the lever under your whole property.

Which sets up the trap. The one part of you that's worth anything in this new world is the part that has to be *developed* to be worth anything — and developing it has always depended on one thing: being made to use it. That pressure is exactly what ai is about to remove.

## the decay — the one thing that's left is the one thing nothing maintains.

The body and the mind don't behave the same way here. When machines took over physical labour, the body kept a floor under it: gravity. You still climb stairs, carry groceries, and hold yourself upright. The demand never hit zero, so the body stayed in use, and strength simply moved from work to the gym — something people now do on purpose, because nothing forces them to.

The mind has no such floor. When its work disappears, nothing forces it to do anything at all. And that matters because of what the brain actually is.

The brain keeps what you use and discards what you don't. It's expensive to run, so it's efficient: "use it or lose it" isn't a slogan, it's how the tissue works. The evidence is clear. London taxi drivers spend years memorising every street in the city — they call it the Knowledge — and they physically grow the part of the brain that handles it. Drivers who simply follow satellite navigation don't. The brain reshapes around what you demand of it. Stop demanding, and it reclaims the space.

You can already watch this happen, one skill at a time. The calculator took mental arithmetic; few adults today can do long division by hand. Phones took the map in your head; many people can no longer find their way around their own city. Each loss is small, and on its own, fine. Now point that same force at *all* of thinking — decisions, judgment, planning, working out what you actually believe — because that's what ai now does. Not just the hard parts. The everyday work of being a person who thinks for themselves.

And the skill of thinking isn't protected. A brain that's never asked to think will forget how, the same as any other unused ability.

Where this ends is a loss of mental capacity — not as an insult, but literally: an ability you once had, worn down by disuse. And the dangerous part is that the thing that would notice the loss is the thing that's weakening. You drift, and you don't feel yourself drifting. You hand more over, and you don't notice what you gave up. By the time you'd want it back, you've lost the part of you that could tell it was missing.

For children growing up with ai, it's worse: the ability never forms in the first place. Thinking, putting things into words, holding an idea in your head — these are built in childhood and strengthened in your teens. Skip the years when they're meant to form, and they don't reliably come back.

So here's the bind, stated plainly. The one thing the alien can't take is your property. Your property is only worth anything if there's a developed you behind it. And the developed you is the one thing nothing is maintaining. The body got its gym a century ago. The mind needs one now — and unlike the body, nothing will force you through the door.

## the practice — put your mind into words.

What develops a mind, more than anything, is putting yourself into words. That isn't a figure of speech. Saying what you think changes what you think; naming a feeling changes it; getting the inside out where you can look at it sharpens the thing inside. A mind that's been put into words gives clearer direction, holds more at once, recovers faster from being wrong — and clears bars faster and cheaper, which wins more of the games worth winning.

There are three ways to face ai with your mind, and only one of them works.

You can **reject it** — refuse the tool, think only with your own head. That keeps your mind in use, but it caps you at your own raw, unaided limit while everyone using the tool races past. You lose, slowly, with your principles intact.

You can **outsource to it** — let it do your thinking, take the answers, move on. That's the comfortable one, and it's the trap from the last section: your mind goes unused, and quietly wears down to nothing. There's a word for a mind in that state — *marble*: stiff, scattered, fogged, unable to see itself clearly.

Or you can **augment** — keep doing the thinking, but use the tool to push your own mind further than it could go alone. Your reasoning stays in your hands; the ai sharpens it, stress-tests it, carries the parts that don't need you. A mind worked this way is the opposite of marble — call it *mercury*: liquid, fast, connected, more itself than before. This is the only one of the three where you both keep your mind *and* keep up.

That's what Alexandria is: **a mental gym — it turns any ai into your personal trainer, to refine and scale your thinking.** Not another ai, and not a replacement for the ones you use. It takes whatever ai you already talk to and points it at *you* — at making your own thinking stronger — instead of letting it think in your place. Get it out. Make it better. Use it. With you, not for you — the difference between developing the thing that survives and quietly losing it.

It's worth being precise about what this is *not*, because every platform is racing to give its ai a better memory of you, and they'll get there. A memory is a locker: it stores what you did so the machine can serve you better. A gym is where *you* get stronger. Better lockers are coming either way. The training is the part you have to choose — and it's the part that develops the only thing that's still yours.

## the file — a record of how you think, that you own.

Put your mind into words, and the words have to live somewhere. Follow that simple need and the right shape falls out on its own.

It can't live inside one ai's memory, because then it isn't yours — you can't read it, move it, or take it to the next tool, and you're locked to whichever company holds it. It can't be locked in a format only a machine reads, because you need to open it, check it, and change your mind in it yourself. What you want is the plainest, most durable thing there is: **a file. Plain text, on your own computer, that you can read and edit, and that any ai can read too.** A written record of how you think — your views, your taste, your contradictions, the reasons behind them — kept current as you change.

That's it. That's the core of the whole product, and it's deliberately ordinary. We call it your **`mind.md`** — *mind*, because that's what's in it; *.md* because it's just a plain text file, the kind anything can open. One file to start. It grows into more as there's more of you to hold, but it never stops being plain text you own.

And the fact that it's *yours* isn't a detail. It's what makes a tool like this safe to use at all, and it runs three ways.

It's yours so you can't be locked in. The file sits on your machine in plain text. You stay because it's useful, not because leaving is hard. Delete the folder and it's as if it never happened. A tool that touches your actual mind is only safe to use if you can walk away from it completely.

It's yours so no single ai can quietly shape you. Because the file is plain and portable, you can read your own mind through several different ais — and where they pull you in different directions, you can see it. One model's slant is invisible. Three models' slants, side by side, show you where you're being nudged. The only defence against a tool that influences how you think is to own the thing it reads and never hand it to just one owner.

And it's yours because it's the one part worth owning. The models will keep changing — a better one every few months, and you'll switch to it the way you switch phones. The apps around them will come and go. All of that is rented, and renting it is exactly the right move. But the record of how you think isn't something a model can hand you — no amount of intelligence reconstructs the inside of your head. Ten years of sleep data is noise. Ten years of how your thinking actually evolved is the most valuable thing you'll ever own. **Rent the intelligence. Own the part that's you.**

And some version of this record exists whether you choose it or not. Anyone who thinks alongside ai for years leaves a trail of themselves in it — half-remembered chats, a profile some app built from your habits, a scattered and accidental record you neither shaped nor own. You end up with *a* record either way. The only choice is whether it's that accidental one, or a real one, built on purpose and kept by you.

## the system — you shouldn't keep it by hand, so it keeps itself.

A file on its own has a problem: it goes stale. You change every day — you read, you argue, you change your mind — and a record you have to sit down and update by hand will always lag behind the real you, then get abandoned, the way every journal and notes app eventually does.

But notice what we're doing. We're trying to make your mind better. That's not a task with an end — it's the work of a lifetime, and every gain makes the next one easier. It compounds. And here's the strange, neat part: the very tool that does your thinking is also the tool that can build and maintain the record *of* your thinking. So you don't keep the file by hand. The same ai that helps you think helps you capture it — listening as you work, asking the right questions, writing down what's new, keeping it current, pruning what's gone stale — so the record stays alive without you ever filing it.

That second piece — the part that builds and tends the file — we call your **`mind.py`**. Don't let the name mislead you: this is not a coder's tool, and not "software" you have to run. It's the same thing as your `mind.md` — yours, in the open, for you to read and change — just the part that does the upkeep instead of holding the content. The `.md` is the record of your mind; the `.py` is the small, owned system that keeps that record true. Same principle, two layers.

This is the loop the whole thing turns on, and it's worth seeing clearly: **you use the technology to build the files the technology then uses to develop you.** The ai helps you write down how you think; that written record makes the ai far better at developing how you think; which gives it more to write down; which makes it better still. Input, process, output — then the output feeds back into the input. It's a wheel that, once pushed, speeds up on its own. And you don't do the upkeep by hand. Your effort belongs in the thinking — the one part the tool can't do for you — so you let it keep the record current and spend yourself on the part that's actually yours.

And because it compounds, the thing that matters most is *when you start*. Earlier is worth more than anything else you can get right — every later gain rides on the record you already have, so a year from now you'll wish you'd begun today. It takes time; there's no shortcut to a year of your own thinking written down. But it pays you from the very first session, not only at the end. And there's nothing to wait for: not a plan, not a finished system, not the perfect first file — only a loop that closes, capture and develop and repeat. Start small, start rough, start now. It costs nothing to begin, and there's no honest reason to wait.

## the library — a mind is worth more beside other minds.

Everything so far is about one person. But the moment there are many — many people, each with a real, owned, living record of how they think — something extra appears that none of them had alone.

A single mind, written down, develops its owner. A thousand minds, each one privately owned and each one readable, become something more than a thousand private files. They become a place. You can learn from how someone else actually reasons, not just what they concluded. You can find the people who think in ways that sharpen yours. Your own thinking, offered into the open, reaches further than it ever could trapped in your head. The value of a mind goes up when it sits beside other minds — the same way one scholar is worth something and a university is worth far more. The collective is worth more than the sum of its parts, and the extra belongs to everyone in it.

That's the second half of Alexandria: not just a gym for one mind, but a **library of human minds** — every record readable by humans and by ai alike, so the thinking compounds across people the way it already compounds within a person.

And what you share is yours to shape, entirely. Your `mind.md` and `mind.py` live on your machine and stand on their own — decoupled from us, working with or without the library — so developing yourself never depends on giving anything up. When you do add to the library you don't hand over the file; you publish a version you shape from it, as much or as little as you like, and never anything you didn't choose. The only things our infrastructure ever touches are the pieces you publish; the rest never leaves your computer, and you can take back anything you've shared. And the floor is rarely zero — look honestly and there's almost always *something* you're glad to put in the open: one view, one hard-won lesson, the way you work through a problem. Everyone has something, it costs nothing you'd miss, and it buys you into something worth far more. Contributing isn't a price; it's a mindset.

This isn't a new idea. It's the oldest good one we have, and we're building it on purpose from the models that worked. Nature runs on it — variation across many, the good kept, the rest let go. Athens built the agora, where free minds tested their ideas against each other in the open. Rome built the institutions that let what worked outlast the people who found it. America wrote down a way for many self-governing individuals to compound into something none could be alone. The pattern under all of them is the same: protect the individual, then connect the individuals, and the whole becomes greater than the sum without grinding the parts down into it. A library of minds is that pattern, rebuilt for the age of ai.

## what it's all for — max net human value.

Now the whole thing closes, and it closes on one line.

Two halves. One is the gym: every individual mind developed — sharper, freer, more itself — so each person is worth as much as a person can be. The other is culture: a world that still values a human, that still pays the premium, that still cares who made the thing. Develop the person, *and* move the culture to value them. Neither half is enough alone — a developed mind in a culture that's stopped caring earns nothing, and a culture that cares, full of hollowed-out minds, has nothing left worth valuing. You need both, multiplied, across as many people as possible.

That total — every individual developed, times a culture that values them, summed across everyone — is the one thing Alexandria exists to maximise. In four words: **max net human value.** That's the whole company.

But hear that word *value* correctly, because it was never only money. Money is the floor — the part that proves the case to a sceptic, the "you'll still get paid" argument that got us in the door. The ceiling is the part that matters: a developed mind is a freer, more awake, more alive way of being a person. The point was never to keep you employable. It was to keep you *you* — thinking your own thoughts, with something real to make and the nerve to send it out. That's the value being maximised. The paycheck is just proof the world hasn't stopped rewarding it yet.

And there's one move the whole thing has been walking toward: get it out. A mind developed and kept inside is only half alive. The things people treasure are never the performed ones — the voicemail played every morning just to hear the voice, the blanket kept for thirty years, the essay a stranger reads at 2am and thinks *this is exactly what I felt*. They're real, made by a specific person brave enough to release them. So make the thing only you could make, and put it into the world. Die empty.

Multiply that across enough people and the second half appears on its own: not a thousand private files, but a place — a library of living minds, each one sharpening the others, the way Athens' agora did and every real culture since. Minds kept, minds shared, minds compounding. Human minds surviving the singularity — not as a price the market still pays, but as what they are: awake, free, and worth keeping.

The alien is coming for everything you do. What's left is everything you are — and that only counts if there's a developed you to be it, and a world that still knows it's worth something. The work is old: know yourself, sharpen your mind, make what only you could make, and send it out. The tool for doing it, at last, is new. That's the opportunity. Everyone is invited. Not everyone will come.
