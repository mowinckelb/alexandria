# alexandria.

## what's left for us?

Intelligence is being industrialized — made cheap, made fast, made at scale. Add robots for the body, and you get the claim this whole decade runs on: whatever you do, a machine will do, and do it better. You don't have to believe it yet; the question it forces stands either way.

And it isn't rhetorical: when a machine can do everything you do, and do it better, what are people for?

This is a straight attempt to answer that, from the ground up — what's actually happening, why it's more dangerous than it looks, where humans still get paid, how to play it, and what to do about it.

*This is the full argument. For a shorter, more personal version, read [the letter](/docs/letter.pdf).*

---

## the premise.

Start with the part that isn't really in dispute anymore.

In 1951, Alan Turing — one of the founders of modern computing — said that once machines could think, we should "expect the machines to take control." He treated it as a question of when, not if. Seventy-five years later, the field largely agrees with him. In 2023, Geoffrey Hinton, whose work underlies nearly every AI system running today, left Google so he could say plainly that AI is on track to beat humans at most things, perhaps everything, and that we are not ready for it.

This isn't hype. AI runs on the same basic principle as the brain: networks of artificial neurons that strengthen the connections between them through experience, exactly as the neurons in your head do. We didn't stumble onto that — we copied it from biology on purpose, and it worked. And once you take a principle out of biology and rebuild it in silicon, it stops obeying biology's limits. A bird is remarkable, but no bird flies at three times the speed of sound. A jet isn't a better bird; it's a different thing with a higher ceiling. AI is that same move applied to thinking, and robotics is that move applied to the body. The ceiling rises with every release, and there is no law of physics that says it stops anywhere near you.

You're free to bet against this. Just know what you're betting against: more or less every government, every large company, and most of the smartest people alive, all pushing the same way with all their money. There is no wall — no missing piece, no law, no obstacle — standing between here and there. This isn't a matter of faith. It's simply where almost all the effort is pointed.

So take the strong version, because that's where it gets interesting: **AI and robots will match or beat humans at everything humans do.** Everything from here is about what's left when that's true.

## what a human is.

To work out what's left, count what a person actually brings. It's a short list: four capabilities and one property.

The four capabilities are **intelligence, empathy, strength, and dexterity** — brain, heart, legs, and hands. That's the whole list, and it has to be, because everything a person can do is either mind or body. There's no third place for a skill to live. Anything you might add — creativity, leadership, taste — breaks down into some mix of those four when you look closely.

And all four are getting cheap, one after another. Strength went first, a century ago, when machines outmatched any workforce. Intelligence is going now — the memo, the model, the analysis. Dexterity is next, as robots gain good hands. Even empathy goes: a model trained on billions of conversations becomes warm enough that most people, most of the time, accept the warmth without looking behind it. Much of what feels like heart is really intelligence in another form.

The fifth thing is different. It's not something you *do* — it's something you *are*: the simple fact that a given thing was done by **you**.

Here's the clearest way to see it. Imagine aliens arrive, and every one of them is better than you at all four capabilities — sharper, stronger, more precise, more caring. So ask yourself: if the alien opened a restaurant, would you eat there? If it were your therapist, would you book the session? For most meals and most sessions, yes — it would be better. So the real question is what you would still do *yourself*, knowing the alien does it better.

You'd still cook dinner for someone you love. Still hug your child. Still write the toast for your friend's wedding. Still stand at your father's grave for a while. The alien can have every capability there is. It can't be one of us — and for a whole category of things, that's the only thing that matters.

Picture the alien weaving a blanket identical to your grandmother's. Same wool, same pattern, no test could tell them apart. It still isn't the same blanket. The value was never in the wool; it was in whose hands made it. The moment a machine makes it, the thing you cared about is gone. This works at two levels: *a human made this* is worth something, and *this specific human made this* — your grandmother, your friend, you — is worth far more.

So that's the shape of what's left. The four capabilities compete on the thing that gets made, and there the alien wins. The fifth competes on who made it, and there the alien can't compete at all — by definition, because the moment it does the thing, the thing stops being what you valued. That's the one part of you that can't be copied. It's also the part you're about to lose.

## the decay.

Being yourself sounds safe. It isn't, and here's the part most people miss: it only counts if it's *developed*, and the thing that would develop it is exactly what AI is taking away.

The body and the mind don't behave the same way here. When machines took over physical labor, the body kept a floor under it: gravity. You still climb stairs, carry groceries, and hold yourself upright. The demand never hit zero, so the body stayed in use, and strength simply moved from work to the gym — something people now do on purpose, because nothing forces them to.

The mind has no such floor. When its work disappears, nothing forces it to do anything at all. And that matters because of what the brain actually is.

The brain keeps what you use and discards what you don't. It's expensive to run, so it's efficient: "use it or lose it" isn't a slogan, it's how the tissue works. The evidence is clear. London taxi drivers spend years memorizing every street in the city — they call it the Knowledge — and they physically grow the part of the brain that handles it. Drivers who simply follow satellite navigation don't. The brain reshapes around what you demand of it. Stop demanding, and it reclaims the space.

You can already watch this happen, one skill at a time. The calculator took mental arithmetic; few adults today can do long division by hand. Phones took the map in your head; many people can no longer find their way around their own city. Each loss is small, and on its own, fine. Now point that same force at *all* of thinking — decisions, judgment, planning, working out what you actually believe — because that's what AI now does. Not just the hard parts. The everyday work of being a person who thinks for themselves.

And the skill of thinking isn't protected. A brain that's never asked to think will forget how, the same as any other unused ability.

Where this ends is a loss of mental capacity — not as an insult, but literally: an ability you once had, worn down by disuse. And the dangerous part is that the thing that would notice the loss is the thing that's weakening. You drift, and you don't feel yourself drifting. You hand more over, and you don't notice what you gave up. By the time you'd want it back, you've lost the part of you that could tell it was missing.

For children growing up with AI, it's worse: the ability never forms in the first place. Thinking, putting things into words, holding an idea in your head — these are built in childhood and strengthened in your teens. Skip the years when they're meant to form, and they don't reliably come back.

So that's why being yourself is in trouble, and not in some abstract way: you can't win on being yourself if there's no developed self there to be. Hold onto that — the last two sections depend on it. First, the money.

## where humans get paid.

The alien beats you at all four capabilities. So what will anyone pay a human to do?

Start with why this is hard. The work in any job — any job — is just those four capabilities in some mix, and nothing else: intelligence, empathy, strength, dexterity. The alien has more of all four. So on the work itself, head to head, you lose every time. "Follow your passion" doesn't change that. To find where a human still gets paid, you have to find the places where the job *isn't* settled on the work alone.

There are three, and they come down to the shape of the game you're playing. A restaurant is the easiest place to see them, because a restaurant isn't one job — it's a stack of them: prep, cook, plate, serve, host, clean, manage. Run each through the test.

**Maximisation games — the alien just wins.** Here more is always better, with no ceiling: speed on the tickets, volume at the dish station, squeezing the recipe for all it's worth. Every scrap of extra capability pays, forever, because the returns never flatten. And the work is just those four capabilities, every one of which the alien has more of. So it wins outright, and a human in the seat is only a bottleneck. Don't play these for money.

**Threshold games — clear the bar, and it's a tie.** Most of the economy is here. There's a hard line, and nothing above it counts: the kitchen is clean enough to pass inspection, the food is safe, the contract is valid, the car got you there in one piece. A spotless kitchen or a safer-than-safe meal earns you nothing extra. The alien clears the bar — but so do you, with AI helping you over it. And once you've both cleared it, you're even on the work. The moment you're even on the work, only one question is left: does anyone care that a *human* did it?

That question is what's left to play for, so hold onto it. The alien waiter remembers every name, reads every table, never has an off night. Most nights you'd take it — honestly, better service. But the anniversary dinner, or the place where the owner knows your family, and you want the person. Same job, same bar, opposite answer — because how much you care that a human did it has changed.

**Diminishing games — a mix of the two.** This shape is variable, part bar and part climb. You're maximising — more capability keeps adding real value — but the gains taper, until past a point they shrink to nothing, because the person you're serving can no longer tell the difference. A 99% sear and a 100% sear are the same sear to the one eating it, so the skill to close that last gap is worth nothing. While the curve is still climbing, more capability genuinely wins, and there the alien is simply better. Where it has flattened, you're as good as it is, and it's back to whether a human did it. How soon a game flattens — how much of it is fixed bar and how much is open climb — varies from one to the next. You tie where you can reach the flat part, leaning on AI to get there; you fall behind where you can't, most of all in real time — the live sear, the conversation, the performance — where there's no pausing to use the tool and the alien just performs at its level.

So once you're past the bar — a threshold cleared, or a diminishing game out where more has stopped mattering — you and the alien are even on the work, and the winner comes down to whether people care a human did it. Those ties are the games worth playing. So how does a human actually win them?

It comes down to two numbers that people constantly blur together. The first is the **cost difference**: how much more you cost than the alien to do the same job — your time, your wage, the fact that you sleep. The second is the **premium**: how much extra people will pay *because* a human did it. You win when the **premium beats the cost difference** — when what people pay for your hand on the work is more than what your hand costs over the machine's. Keep the two on opposite sides of the table; mixing them is where the thinking goes wrong.

Two things lift the premium. The first is closeness: a stranger's blanket is worth barely more than a machine's, but your grandmother's is worth almost anything — the tighter the bond between you and the maker, the higher the premium. The second is culture: a society chooses what it cares about, and one that values the human hand pays for it, while one that doesn't automates and pockets the difference. The premium isn't fixed by physics; it's a decision. (Lifting that decision, across a whole culture, is the larger project Alexandria is part of.)

And there's one lever on the other number, the one you control. The more developed you are, the faster and cheaper you clear the bar, and the smaller your cost difference — which opens up more games where the premium beats it. Remember that; it's where the last section pays off.

Then there are the games decided by neither capability nor premium, but by rule. Some simply declare what kind of player is allowed. Sport is the clean case: the event exists *because* a human is doing it, so a robot sprinter doesn't win the race, it ends it. The rule cuts the other way too — a drone race or a robot olympics bars humans. And chess is the proof that the human premium is real, and growing: engines passed the best human in 1997 and now sit 900 points beyond any human's reach, yet human chess has never been bigger, and nobody pays to watch two engines play. The unbeatable machine has sat there for years and made the human game *more* valuable, not less.

One move ties all of this together, and it's the one to walk away with. The alien has been your rival across the table this whole time — but the same AI is also a tool in your hand. It's both at once. As your rival, it competes for the job; as your tool, you aim it at the work and take the credit, the way every tool has always worked — the chef didn't grow the wheat, the writer didn't make the pen, and the cook with the better oven still gets the credit for the meal. So the winning play in every tied game is one and the same: use the AI to clear the bar cheaply, then collect the premium for being the human who did. You don't out-work the alien. You put it to work, and win on the one thing it can never own.

Last thing, because it gets confused constantly. All of this is about *money* — what someone will pay a human for. It says nothing about what you should *do*. Cook, write, run, and paint for free for the rest of your life, simply because you want to. The next section isn't about the meaning of life. It's about how to play the games you can win.

## how to play it.

If the last section is the map, this is the route. A few rules fall straight out of it.

**Don't play maximisation games for money.** If a game has no ceiling and nobody cares who did it — raw speed, raw volume, raw accuracy — the alien wins and you're a bottleneck. Hand it over and move up.

**Play where being yourself pays.** Threshold, diminishing, and human-reserved games — anything where, once the work is good enough, people care that a human did it. The waiter at the anniversary dinner, the chef whose restaurant you love, the teacher, the therapist, the founder whose name is the brand. Choose those on purpose.

**You don't have to be the best — just good enough for someone.** This is the part that frees people. You don't need to clear the bar for the whole world, because the premium rises as the circle tightens. Not good enough to compete in your field? Be good enough for your town. Not your town? Your family. Not your family? Yourself — and at the center you always clear the bar, because the audience is you, and a developed person makes even that worth doing. The grandmother isn't a professional knitter. The blanket she made is the most valuable one her granddaughter owns.

**Use AI as the tool, not the replacement.** Use it to clear the bar cheaply — let it do the execution, the research, the drafts — and keep the role where the human premium pays. The line is the direction of the arrow: you give the intent, it does the work. The moment it gives the intent and you do the work, you've swapped places, and you're the tool.

None of this runs on autopilot, though. Picking the right games, giving good direction, knowing when something is actually right — all of it depends on having a clear, developed sense of who you are and what you think. Which is exactly the thing AI is quietly eroding, and exactly the thing the last section is about.

## the practice.

So here's where it lands. The one thing that survives — being yourself — is also the thing you have to *develop*, and AI is busy removing the pressure that would develop it. The body got a gym when machines took the labor. The mind needs one now, and it's more urgent, because the body had gravity to keep it honest and the mind has nothing.

What develops it, more than anything, is putting yourself into words. That isn't a figure of speech. Saying what you think changes what you think; naming a feeling changes it; getting the inside out where you can look at it sharpens the thing inside. A mind that has been examined gives clearer direction, holds more at once, and recovers faster from being wrong. That's also the cheaper, faster operator from a few sections back — the one with the small cost difference who wins more of the games worth winning.

That's what Alexandria is: **a gym for the mind — it turns any AI into a personal trainer that helps you refine and scale your thinking.** Not another AI, and not a replacement for the ones you use. The way it works is simple: you put your mind into writing — your views, your taste, your contradictions, kept current and owned by you — so any AI can read it. That written record is the foundation, and it does two things at once: it gives the AI something to train *you* against, instead of thinking for you, and it makes every AI you use work *with* you instead of treating you like a stranger. With you, not for you — that's the whole difference between developing the thing that survives and quietly losing it.

The setup is simple, and the reason it's built this way matters: the files are yours. Plain text, on your own machine, with no lock-in. You stay because it's useful, not because leaving is difficult. And because no single AI owns the files, you can read your own mind through several of them, which stops any one model from shaping you in ways you can't see. That's not a minor detail. A tool that touches your actual mind is only safe to use if it's yours.

It's also the one part worth owning. The models will keep changing — a better one every few months, and you'll switch to it the way you switch phones. The apps around them will come and go. All of that is rented, and that's fine; renting the intelligence is exactly the right move. But the record of how *you* think isn't something a model can hand you — no amount of intelligence reconstructs the inside of your head. It's the one piece that's yours to keep while everything around it gets replaced. So that's the piece to own. Rent the intelligence. Own the part that's you.

And to be clear, some version of this happens to you whether you choose it or not. Anyone who thinks alongside AI for years leaves a trail of themselves in it — half-remembered chats, a profile some app built from your habits, a scattered and accidental record of your thinking that you neither shaped nor own. You end up with *a* record either way. The only choice is whether it's that accidental one, or a real one, developed on purpose and kept by you. Drift gives you the first by default. The second you have to choose.

That's the argument. The alien is coming for everything you do. What's left is everything you are — and that only counts if there's a developed you to be. The work is old. The tool for doing it is new. That's the opportunity.
