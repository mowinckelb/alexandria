### 1. Byung-Chul Han — The Agony of Eros
**Source:** Byung-Chul Han, *The Agony of Eros* (MIT Press, 2017); featured in Aeon essay Jan 2026

**Delta location:** Love.md — extends de Rougemont (landed 03-29) with a structural diagnosis. De Rougemont: passion needs the obstacle. Han: the deeper problem is narcissism eliminating Otherness — you cannot desire someone you have fused with. This reframes the independence model with Khiara: loving that she is genuinely Other is not just a relational preference — it is preserving the structural precondition for eros. Han also names what the fish love problem (Love.md) actually is: not selfishness, but narcissism collapsing the difference between self and Other until only the self remains. The "inferno of the same" is why the modern model produces desire decay — not because it selects for the wrong prerequisite, but because the merger dynamic destroys the Otherness that eros requires.

**Fragment:**
> "The crisis of love does not derive from too many others so much as the erosion of the Other. The narcissification of the Self destroys the Other by rendering all difference into the Same."

> "Depression plunges the subject into itself, while Eros makes possible the experience of the Other's otherness, which leads the self out of a narcissistic inferno. It sets into motion freely willed self-renunciation — a singular process of weakening that is nonetheless accompanied by a feeling of strength."

> "We no longer fall in love and risk madness, but enter into a relationship of mutual consent in which we seek only 'the confirmation of oneself in the Other.' Love has become a mixture of hedonistic calculation and stress relief — lacking all transcendence and transgression."

### 2. "Celebrated Otherness" — Muise & Goss
**Source:** Amy Muise & Goss, "Does Too Much Closeness Dampen Desire?" *Current Directions in Psychological Science*, 2024

**Delta location:** Love.md — empirical support for the independence model. The constitution says: I love that we're so different, she is her own independent person — this is a feature, not a bug. Muise & Goss find exactly this in the data: people who "celebrated otherness" — who valued and cultivated partner distinctiveness — reported higher sexual desire. This names the mechanism linking the independence model to eros-building: preserving the partner as genuinely Other is the structural condition for sustaining desire. Connects Han (inferno of the same) + de Rougemont (passion needs the obstacle) to the specific relationship architecture already described in Love.md. The theoretical model now has empirical backing.

**Fragment:**
> "When couples were asked about factors that help maintain sexual desire, autonomy was identified as a key theme — including 'physical distance, having personal projects that do not include the partner or a more psychological sense of otherness — recognizing the partner as a separate person.'"

> "People who reported higher 'celebrated otherness' in their relationship, in which partner distinctiveness is valued and cultivated, reported higher sexual desire."

### 3. Michael Polanyi — The Tacit Dimension (1966)
**Source:** Michael Polanyi, *The Tacit Dimension* (Doubleday, 1966), Chapter 1

**Delta location:** Mind.md — philosophical formalization of the entire cognitive architecture. The Author has the operational model (conductor, 51%, hazy fragments, blue note). Polanyi provides the epistemological grounding: this is not a productivity framework, it is the fundamental structure of all human knowing. The subsidiary/focal distinction maps exactly onto sub-symbolic/symbolic. The from–to structure (attend FROM subsidiary awareness FOR focal awareness) is the philosophical description of what the constitution calls the tacit substrate underneath the symbolic touchpoint. "All knowledge is either tacit or rooted in tacit knowledge. More touchpoints at 51 beats fewer at 95. The conductor does not need more notes — she needs the right tacit awareness to know when notes are wrong.

**Fragment:**
> "We can know more than we can tell. Take an example. We know a person's face, and can recognize it among a thousand, indeed among a million. Yet we usually cannot tell how we recognize a face we know."

> "In an act of tacit knowing we attend **from** something **for** attending to something else."

> "When I use a hammer to drive in a nail, I attend to both nail and hammer, but in a different way. I watch the effect of my strokes on the nail and try to wield the hammer so as to hit the nail most effectively. I have a **subsidiary awareness** of the feeling of the hammer in the palm of my hand which is merged into the **focal awareness** of my driving in the nail."

### 4. Timur Kuran — Preference Falsification (1995)
**Source:** Timur Kuran, *Private Truths, Public Lies* (Harvard UP, 1995); "Now Out of Never," *World Politics*, 1991

**Delta location:** Love.md — names the mechanism of the coordination problem. The constitution already identified the trap: rejecting the modern dating model leaves you right and alone, because the Nash equilibrium requires others to also defect. Kuran names this precisely: preference falsification creates stable equilibria where everyone publicly endorses what many privately reject, sustaining "through acts of preference falsification pressures that they themselves created." This is exactly the situation: everyone performs belief in the spark because they assume everyone else believes it, even if many privately doubt it. The threshold cascade: the modern dating consensus may not be as stable as it appears. A single cascade — enough people revealing private rejection simultaneously — can collapse it without warning. "Among its predictions is that political revolutions will inevitably continue to catch the world by surprise." The coordination problem is real, but it has a dynamic — not a permanent equilibrium.

**Fragment:**
> "Preference falsification is the selection of a public preference that differs from one's private preference."

> "At any given equilibrium, public opinion may differ from private opinion. In fact, the equilibrium may owe its existence and stability largely to preference falsification on the part of people unsympathetic to the policies it makes possible. Such disgruntled people, even if they form a huge majority, will refrain from dissenting because of social pressures — pressures that **they themselves sustain through acts of preference falsification**."

> "By suppressing their antipathies to the political status quo, the East Europeans **misled everyone, including themselves**, as to the possibility of a successful uprising. In effect, they conferred on their privately despised governments an aura of invincibility. Under the circumstances, public opposition was poised to grow explosively if ever enough people lost their fear of exposing their private preferences."

### 5. Marcus Aurelius — Prokheiron (c. 170 AD)
**Source:** *Meditations* Books II & V (trans. George Long; Farquharson)

**Delta location:** Mind.md — delivers the parked fragment from 03-29. The Meditations as training log for the parallel-not-stacked system. The key is the technical term *prokheiron* (πρόχειρον) — "ready to hand." "Have this thought ready" in V.1 is a translation of this. The Meditations are not journaling or retrospective reflection. They are pre-loading: Marcus writes what he will encounter and how he will respond BEFORE encountering it. The writing IS the rehearsal — the symbolic layer programming itself before the sub-symbolic triggers. This is the oldest extant training protocol for parallel-not-stacked: evidence the framework is not a modern invention but a rediscovery of something the examined life has always done. "Souls are dyed by thoughts" is the operational principle: the symbolic layer becomes sub-symbolic through repetition.

**Fragment:**
> "Begin the morning by saying to thyself, I shall meet with the busy-body, the ungrateful, arrogant, deceitful, envious, unsocial. All these things happen to them by reason of their ignorance of what is good and evil. But I who have seen the nature of the good… can neither be injured by any of them." (II.1)

> "At dawn of day, when you dislike being called, have this thought ready: 'I am called to man's labour; why then do I make a difficulty if I am going out to do what I was born to do?'" (V.1)

> "Your mind will take on the character of your most frequent thoughts: souls are dyed by thoughts." (V.16)

The writing is the training. Not reflection — *prokheiron*: pre-loaded, ready before the situation arrives.

> "Not a dancer but a wrestler: waiting, poised and dug in, for sudden assaults." (VII.61 — Gregory Hays)

The wrestler's stance is *prokheiron* as posture: the prepared body, not the reactive scramble. Seneca makes the mechanics explicit: "Rehearse them in your mind: exile, torture, war, shipwreck. All the terms of our human lot should be before our eyes." The Meditations are that rehearsal in writing.

### 6. Japanese koi (恋) → jō (情) — The Named Transition
**Source:** Japanese literary tradition; *The Tale of Genji* (Murasaki Shikibu, c. 1010)

**Delta location:** Love.md — cross-cultural naming for the transition the Eros Gap describes. Japanese distinguishes 恋 (*koi*) — passionate romantic longing, infatuation, the spark — from 情 (*jō*, also 愛情 *aijō*) — the accumulated affective warmth that deepens through shared life. The *Tale of Genji* documents koi transforming into jō as relationships unfold over time. This is not the agape → eros direction Benjamin is building toward — it is the adjacent phenomenon: a tradition that names the deepening warmth as a distinct, valid love category, not a failed version of the spark. Western tradition collapsed these into one word ("love") and then demanded the koi version as the only real thing. The Japanese tradition suggests the deepening warmth was always real, always named. Medium delta: the Eros Gap may be partly a linguistic trap — a gap that exists in English but is already bridged in Japanese.

**Fragment:**
> **恋が冷めても情が残る** (*Koi ga samete mo jō ga nokoru*) — "Even when romantic love cools, affection/attachment remains."

> "恋 (*koi*): the feeling of falling in love, longing for someone, being unable to stop thinking about them — the butterflies, passion, emotional highs of infatuation. 情 (*jō*): the deep emotional register that grows between people through shared time and experience; the capacity to feel genuinely moved by the passing of things."

> "In the *Tale of Genji*, *koi* (romantic longing and passion) repeatedly transforms into 情 — an accumulated emotional residue of closeness, regret, and tenderness — as relationships unfold and fade."
