## The move I want to name (spine of the talk)

There are two claims that get welded into one, and the whole thing turns on prying them apart.

**Claim A — engineering, true.** Within a regime, loss decreases predictably as you add compute and data. Scaling laws are real. I'm not a denier; I've shipped on the back of them.

**Claim B — political, smuggled.** *Therefore* general intelligence is inevitable, imminent, and the only rational move is to build it first — and *therefore* anyone asking "who does this make powerful" is a romantic standing in front of a train.

B is wearing A's lab coat.

- A is a statement about a curve. B is a statement about the world staying in the regime where the curve held.
- Every S-curve is an exponential seen from inside the knee.
- An extrapolation is not a measurement. It's a bet about *continuation*, and continuation is exactly the thing not yet in evidence.

The engineering earns respect. Then the respect gets loaned to the metaphysics, tax-free. That transfer is the trick. Everything I'm arguing is: **audit the transfer.**

## Why "theology" — earn the word, don't just enjoy it

Not an insult. A structural description. The intelligence-explosion story has the *shape* of an eschatology:

- **An inevitability.** The end is coming whether or not you believe. (Providence.)
- **A discontinuity.** A single moment that changes everything — before/after, the takeoff, the singularity. (The Rapture, with GPUs.)
- **A moral reorganisation around the event.** Given the end is near and total, ordinary concerns — labour, distribution, consent, ecology, who's in the room — become *unserious*. Rearranging deck chairs. (Millenarianism always devalues the present in favour of the imminent absolute.)
- **A budget line.** The one that's new. Belief here is capitalised. The faith has a burn rate.

<!-- the last one is the actual point and the applause line if there is one. don't rush it. the theology is *funded*, and the funding needs the inevitability to be believed, because inevitability is what converts "should we?" into "someone will, so it may as well be us." -->

The function of inevitability: it **retires the question.** If the explosion is coming no matter what, then "who does it make powerful" is not just naive, it's *incoherent* — you don't ask the weather for consent. Inevitability launders a choice into a forecast. That's the work the theology does for the budget.

## What I'm actually asking for (keep small — do not overclaim, they'll pounce)

Not "stop building." I'm not that person and this isn't that talk.

1. **Separate the curve from the creed.** Believe the scaling law exactly as far as the evidence goes, and not one inch into inevitability.
2. **Re-open the retired question.** "Who does it make powerful" is not answered by "it's coming anyway." Even the inevitable has a distribution, and the distribution is a choice.
3. **Watch what the theology excuses.** Every time imminence is used to say a present harm is unserious — labour, consent, ecology, concentration — that's the eschatology doing political work. Name it in the moment.

That's it. Modest on purpose. The immodest version loses the room and I need the room.
