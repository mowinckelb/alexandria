# The Founder Myth

There is a sentence I keep hearing at conferences, on podcasts, in the mouths of people I otherwise respect. It gets said the way you say a thing you believe is kind:

> Nobody can beat you at being you.

~~It's meant as encouragement. It lands on me as a threat.~~ <!-- too fast. slow down. show the machinery before you name the verdict. -->

It's meant as encouragement. It is offered, usually, to someone who is struggling — a reminder that they hold something unrepeatable, that the market cannot commoditise a self. I understand the impulse. I have wanted to say it to people I love.

But sit with the mechanism. **"Nobody can beat you at being you" sounds like freedom and functions like a cage — it tells the lucky they're good and the unlucky they're lacking.** If your particular self happens to be legible to this particular moment — if what you are maps onto what capital currently rewards — then the line is true and flattering and you will repeat it. And if your particular self does not map on, the line is still addressed to you, and now the failure is not the market's, not the era's, not luck's. It's *yours*. You were not enough *you*. You didn't lean in hard enough to the unrepeatable thing.

The line privatises outcome. That is its function, whatever its intent.

## The device

The founder myth is the same move, scaled up.

We are told the story of the individual: the one who saw what others couldn't, who bet everything, who was *right*. The story is load-bearing. It justifies the distribution of the winnings — if the value came from one exceptional person, then the winnings belong to that person, and the hundreds of others (the early engineers, the cleaners, the users whose data trained the thing, the state that funded the underlying research, the parents, the city, the whole substrate) were merely inputs. Fungible. Paid market rate and owed nothing further.

I have watched real work up close for six years. I was one of the first engineers at a company that is now, by the story, the achievement of its founders. And the story is not *false* — they were good, they were early, they held the thing together through years I would not have survived. But the story is not *complete*, and the incompleteness is not innocent. The parts it omits are exactly the parts that would raise the question of who else has a claim.

A myth is not a lie. A myth is a lie about *scope*.

## What luck launders into

Here is the part I have to say against my own comfort, because I have been on the winning side of it.

~~When you succeed, you experience your success from the inside as agency.~~

When you succeed, you experience it from the inside as agency — as a chain of decisions you made, correctly, under uncertainty. That experience is real. You *did* decide. What you cannot see from the inside is the counterfactual: the ten thousand people who decided identically and lost, and are therefore not on stage to describe their process. Survivorship makes skill and luck look identical from the survivor's chair. The founder myth is survivorship bias with a compensation structure attached.

And I want to be careful, because the opposite error is just as lazy. It is *not* true that success is only luck, that effort is theatre, that everyone is equally deserving and the dice do the rest. That's the comfortable cynicism of people who didn't try. Some people really are better at the thing. Effort is real. Judgement is real. I am not smuggling in a flat world where nothing anyone does matters.

I'm saying something narrower and harder to hold: **skill is real AND the story we tell about skill is a device for deciding who gets paid.** Both. The line "nobody can beat you at being you" is not wrong because selves aren't distinct — they are. It's wrong because it converts a fact about distinctness into a verdict about desert, and then hands the verdict to the market to enforce.

## The thing I actually want

I don't have a policy in my pocket. This isn't that kind of essay and I distrust the ones that pretend to be — that tack a five-point plan onto a feeling to look responsible.

What I have is a preference for a different story. Not "the collective did it, the individual is nothing" — that story lies about scope in the other direction, and it has its own body count. The truer story is dull and therefore never told: *a lot of people, most of them uncredited, made a thing, and some of them were more central than others, and the amount the central ones took bears almost no relationship to how central they were.*

That story doesn't fit on a poster. It won't encourage anyone at a conference. It has the specific unglamorous shape of the truth.
