# legibility & consent

A system is only legitimate if the people it acts on can see it and could have said no.

Two conditions, both required. **See it** — the workings are visible, not a black box that "just decides." **Could have said no** — refusal was a real, available option, not a formality. A system that fails either one is acting on people rather than for them, however good its outputs.

This is why I care about the dispatch model being interpretable even when the black box scores better. A grid decision that darkens a neighbourhood is being *done to* people who never saw the model and were never asked. Accuracy doesn't buy you consent.

The ecological version, which is really the same claim:

> Monocultures are legible and fragile; guilds are illegible and resilient.

Careful — legibility cuts both ways and I want to keep the tension honest. Legible *to the people inside* the system = consent, good. Legible *to the power above* it = surveilled, capturable, optimised-to-death, fragile. The monoculture is the field the state can read at a glance and tax and blight. The guild is opaque from above and richly transparent from within. So the target isn't "maximise legibility" — it's **legible downward to the governed, illegible upward to the would-be optimiser.** Build guilds. See [[power-not-capability]].

The co-op is an attempt to build a guild. Which is exactly why doing it alone would betray it — a guild of one is just a monoculture with a nicer story.
