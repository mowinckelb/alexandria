# The Mushroom at the End of the World — Tsing

matsutake grows in the ruined, logged-over, industrially-wrecked forests. the thing of value shows up *after* the plantation logic fails, in the mess it leaves.

reactions:
- "collaborative survival" — she means multispecies but it's the co-op word too. survival is a group verb.
- her villain is the plantation: alienated, scalable, one-crop, made of interchangeable parts that don't need each other. my [[Compost/allotment vs plantation]] note but with 300 pages of proof.
- **scalability is the disease, not the goal.** a scalable project is one that grows without changing its relations — and the only way to not change your relations as you grow is to make everything and everyone interchangeable, i.e. dead. things that are alive can't scale cleanly. margin: "!!! this is the [[velocity]] argument from the biology side"
- so the pushback on my own co-op ambition: the second i design it to *scale*, i've designed a plantation. design it to *live* instead. slower, weirder, un-fundable, resilient.

Tsing + Ostrom are basically arguing the same case from two sides. one on institutions, one on ecology. the guild. keep pulling this thread.
