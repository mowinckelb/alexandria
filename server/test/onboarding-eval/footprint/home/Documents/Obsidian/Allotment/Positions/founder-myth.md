# the founder myth

The individual-genius-founder story is corrosive. Real work is collective; the lone-genius narrative is a marketing device that launders luck and extraction into merit.

The line I keep coming back to, the one carrying the Slow Compute draft:

> "Nobody can beat you at being you" sounds like freedom and functions like a cage — it tells the lucky they're good and the unlucky they're lacking.

It's a beautiful sentence and it's poison. Dress it as empowerment and it does two jobs at once: it tells the winner his position is *identity*, not circumstance (so he owes nobody), and it tells the loser his position is *personal lack* (so he can blame nobody but himself). Both halves erase the room — the collaborators, the luck, the public infrastructure, the people who weren't in the room (see [[velocity]]) but paid for the win anyway.

Nobody builds alone. The garage is a stage set. Behind every "solo founder" is a standards body, a public university, a spouse doing the invisible work, a mentor, a market someone else made. The myth's whole function is to make that scaffolding disappear so the returns can concentrate cleanly on one legible name.

The trap I have to name because it's mine right now: I believe all of this and I still want to build the co-op *alone*. See the 1am note. Believing the myth is false does not make me immune to wanting to be the exception. That's not hypocrisy I've resolved — it's hypocrisy I'm living. Naming it is the minimum. Living differently is the actual work and I haven't done it yet.
