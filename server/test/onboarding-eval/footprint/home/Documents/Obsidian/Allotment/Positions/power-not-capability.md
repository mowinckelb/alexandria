# power, not capability

The question is never *can we* — it's *who does it make powerful*.

This is the first move. Before I ask whether a technology works, whether it scales, whether it's impressive, I ask what distribution of power it produces. Capability is the boring axis. Everyone measures capability because capability is legible and fundable. The interesting axis is: after this exists, who can do what to whom that they couldn't before.

"Can we build it" is an engineering question wearing the mask of a moral one. It flatters the room — of course we can, we're smart, look at the benchmark. And while everyone's admiring the benchmark, the actual decision (who gets to point this at whom) gets made by default, by whoever already had the power, off-screen.

Examples I keep:
- Grid ML (my day job): the model can balance load beautifully. *Who* it balances *toward* — which neighbourhoods eat the brownouts — is not a modelling question. It's a power question wearing a modelling costume. I have watched this get decided in a Slack thread.
- The intelligence-explosion story: see [[agi-theology]]. "Can we get to AGI" is doing the same trick at civilisational scale — the capability framing smuggles the whole political question out the back door.

Second-order, always. First-order capability is a distraction the powerful can afford and the rest of us can't. Reason from the distribution, not the demo.

The reflex is the whole thing. When I hear "can we," I now hear a bell.
